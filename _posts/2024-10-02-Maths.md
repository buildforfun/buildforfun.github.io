## Mathematics
Maths is the science of numbers, their operations, interrelation, and abstractions. Areas of maths include:
- Arithmetic: Basic operations with numbers
- Algebra: Use of symbols and letters to represent numbers and quantities
- Geometry: Study of shapes, sizes, and positions
- Trigonometry: Relationships between sides and angles of triangles
- Calculus: Study of continuous change
- Statistics and Probability: Analysis of data and likelihood of events

## Arithmetic and Number Theory

### Exponents and Roots
- Exponent rules:
  - a^m × a^n = a^(m+n)
  - (a^m)^n = a^(mn)
  - (ab)^n = a^n × b^n
- nth root: ∛a = a^(1/3)

### Modular Arithmetic
- Congruence: a ≡ b (mod m) if m divides (a - b)
- Properties:
  - (a + b) mod m = ((a mod m) + (b mod m)) mod m
  - (a × b) mod m = ((a mod m) × (b mod m)) mod m

### Calculus

Calculus is a branch of mathematics that studies continuous change. It provides powerful tools for analyzing rates of change, accumulation, and the behavior of functions. The two main branches of calculus are differential calculus and integral calculus.

#### 1. Foundations

Before diving into calculus, it's important to understand some fundamental concepts:

##### 1.1 Functions

A function is a relation between inputs and outputs where each input is associated with exactly one output.

- Notation: f(x) = ... where x is the input (independent variable) and f(x) is the output (dependent variable)
- Example: f(x) = x² + 3x + 2

##### 1.2 Graphs of Functions

Graphing functions helps visualize their behavior:

- x-axis represents the input
- y-axis represents the output
- Points on the graph are (x, f(x))

##### 1.3 Basic Function Types

- Linear: f(x) = mx + b (m is slope, b is y-intercept)
- Quadratic: f(x) = ax² + bx + c
- Exponential: f(x) = a·bˣ
- Logarithmic: f(x) = log_b(x)
- Trigonometric: sin(x), cos(x), tan(x), etc.

#### 2. Limits

Limits are the foundation of calculus. They describe the behavior of a function as the input approaches a particular value.

##### 2.1 Intuitive Understanding

lim(x→a) f(x) = L means that as x gets arbitrarily close to a, f(x) gets arbitrarily close to L.

##### 2.2 Formal Definition (ε-δ Definition)

For every ε > 0, there exists a δ > 0 such that if 0 < |x - a| < δ, then |f(x) - L| < ε.

##### 2.3 Types of Limits

1. One-sided limits:
   - Left-hand limit: lim(x→a⁻) f(x)
   - Right-hand limit: lim(x→a⁺) f(x)
2. Infinite limits: lim(x→a) f(x) = ∞ or -∞
3. Limits at infinity: lim(x→∞) f(x) or lim(x→-∞) f(x)

### 2.4 Techniques for Evaluating Limits

1. Direct substitution
2. Factoring
3. Rationalization
4. L'Hôpital's Rule (for indeterminate forms, covered later)

### 2.5 Continuity

A function f(x) is continuous at x = a if:
1. f(a) is defined
2. lim(x→a) f(x) exists
3. lim(x→a) f(x) = f(a)

## 3. Derivatives

Derivatives measure the rate of change of a function with respect to its variable.

### 3.1 Definition of a Derivative

The derivative of f(x) is defined as:

f'(x) = lim(h→0) [f(x+h) - f(x)] / h

### 3.2 Interpretation of Derivatives

1. Slope of the tangent line to the graph of f(x) at any point
2. Instantaneous rate of change of f(x) with respect to x
3. Velocity when f(x) represents position as a function of time

### 3.3 Notation

- Leibniz notation: dy/dx
- Lagrange notation: f'(x)
- Newton's notation: ẏ

### 3.4 Rules for Differentiation

1. Power Rule: d/dx [x^n] = nx^(n-1)
2. Constant Rule: d/dx [c] = 0 (where c is a constant)
3. Sum/Difference Rule: d/dx [f(x) ± g(x)] = f'(x) ± g'(x)
4. Product Rule: d/dx [f(x)g(x)] = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)
5. Quotient Rule: d/dx [f(x)/g(x)] = [f'(x)g(x) - f(x)g'(x)] / [g(x)]^2
6. Chain Rule: d/dx [f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) × g'(x)

### 3.5 Derivatives of Common Functions

1. d/dx [e^x] = e^x
2. d/dx [ln(x)] = 1/x
3. d/dx [sin(x)] = cos(x)
4. d/dx [cos(x)] = -sin(x)
5. d/dx [tan(x)] = sec²(x)

### 3.6 Higher-Order Derivatives

- Second derivative: f''(x) or d²y/dx²
- Third derivative: f'''(x) or d³y/dx³

### 3.7 Applications of Derivatives

1. Finding extrema (maximum and minimum values)
2. Optimization problems
3. Related rates
4. Curve sketching
5. L'Hôpital's Rule for evaluating limits

#### 4. Integrals

Integration is the reverse process of differentiation. It allows us to calculate areas, volumes, and accumulations.

##### 4.1 Indefinite Integrals

An indefinite integral is a family of functions whose derivative is the integrand:

∫ f(x) dx = F(x) + C

Here, F(x) is an antiderivative of f(x), and C is the constant of integration.

##### 4.2 Definite Integrals

A definite integral represents the area under the curve of f(x) between x = a and x = b:

∫[a,b] f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)

##### 4.3 Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

1. Part 1: d/dx [∫[a,x] f(t) dt] = f(x)
2. Part 2: ∫[a,b] f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)

This theorem connects differentiation and integration, showing that they are inverse processes.

##### 4.4 Techniques of Integration

1. Basic integrals (power rule, exponentials, trigonometric functions)
2. U-substitution (reverse chain rule)
3. Integration by parts: ∫ u dv = uv - ∫ v du
4. Partial fractions
5. Trigonometric substitution
6. Improper integrals

##### 4.5 Applications of Integration

1. Area between curves
2. Volume of solids of revolution
3. Arc length
4. Surface area
5. Work done by a variable force
6. Center of mass

#### 5. Differential Equations

Differential equations are equations that involve derivatives of an unknown function.

##### 5.1 First-Order Linear Differential Equations

The general form is: dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x)

Solution method:
1. Find the integrating factor: μ(x) = e^∫P(x)dx
2. Multiply both sides by μ(x)
3. Integrate both sides

##### 5.2 Second-Order Linear Differential Equations

The general form is: a(x)y'' + b(x)y' + c(x)y = f(x)

For constant coefficient homogeneous equations:
1. Form the characteristic equation: ar^2 + br + c = 0
2. Find the roots of the characteristic equation
3. Form the general solution based on the nature of the roots

##### 5.3 Applications of Differential Equations

1. Population growth models
2. Radioactive decay
3. Newton's law of cooling
4. Mechanical vibrations
5. Electrical circuits

## Probability and Statistics

### Probability Theory
- Probability of an event: P(A) = (number of favorable outcomes) / (total number of possible outcomes)
- Conditional probability: P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)
- Bayes' theorem: P(A|B) = P(B|A)P(A) / P(B)

### Descriptive Statistics
- Mean: x̄ = (Σ xᵢ) / n
- Variance: s² = Σ(xᵢ - x̄)² / (n - 1)
- Standard deviation: s = √(s²)

### Inferential Statistics
- Central Limit Theorem
- Confidence intervals
- Hypothesis testing

### Hypothesis Testing
- Null hypothesis (H₀) vs. Alternative hypothesis (H₁)
- p-value: probability of obtaining test results at least as extreme as the observed results, assuming that the null hypothesis is correct
- Significance level (α): threshold for rejecting the null hypothesis
