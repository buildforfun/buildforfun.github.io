The history of Sri Lanka is rich and complex, marked by ancient civilizations, colonial rule, and struggles for independence. Here’s a concise overview of key events and developments throughout its history.

## Ancient History

- **Prehistoric Settlements**: Evidence of human habitation in Sri Lanka dates back to around 125,000 years ago, with the Balangoda Man identified as some of the earliest inhabitants. The island's early settlers were hunter-gatherers who later transitioned to agriculture, particularly rice cultivation, around 10,000 BCE[2][7].

- **Arrival of Prince Vijaya**: According to legend, Prince Vijaya arrived from northern India around 543 BCE, marking the beginning of the Sinhalese monarchy and the establishment of the first significant kingdom in Sri Lanka, known as the Pre-Anuradhapura period[4][9].

- **Anuradhapura Period (377 BCE – 1017 CE)**: This era saw the rise of urban settlements and the spread of Buddhism, which became a dominant cultural force. The city of Anuradhapura served as a political and religious center, featuring advanced irrigation systems and monumental architecture[4][5].

## Colonial Era

- **Portuguese Arrival (1505)**: The Portuguese were the first Europeans to arrive in Sri Lanka, establishing control over coastal areas and introducing Christianity. Their rule was marked by conflict with local kingdoms, particularly in Kandy[1][3].

- **Dutch Control (1658)**: The Dutch ousted the Portuguese and took over much of the coastal regions. They imposed heavy taxes but were less aggressive towards Buddhism compared to their predecessors[3][4].

- **British Colonization (1796)**: The British seized control during the Napoleonic Wars, initially administering Ceylon as a crown colony. By 1815, after defeating the Kingdom of Kandy in the Kandyan Wars, they unified the entire island under British rule[1][8].

## Path to Independence

- **Political Developments**: In 1931, Ceylon was granted universal franchise, leading to increased political participation among its citizens. Tensions between ethnic groups began to surface during this period[1][9].

- **Independence (1948)**: On February 4, 1948, Ceylon gained independence from British rule. It became a republic in 1972 and was renamed Sri Lanka[1][9].

## Modern Era

- **Civil Conflict**: The late 20th century was marked by ethnic tensions between Sinhalese and Tamils, culminating in a brutal civil war that lasted from 1983 until 2009. This conflict resulted in significant loss of life and displacement[2][4].

- **Post-War Recovery**: Since the end of the civil war, Sri Lanka has focused on rebuilding its economy and addressing ethnic reconciliation issues while navigating political challenges.

## Conclusion

Sri Lanka's history reflects a blend of indigenous cultures and external influences shaped by colonization and conflict. Today, it stands as a nation rich in cultural heritage and resilience amidst its complex past.

The Sri Lankan Civil War was a complex and devastating conflict that lasted from 1983 to 2009, primarily involving the Sri Lankan government and the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE). Here’s a detailed account of its history, origins, key events, and aftermath:

## Origins of the Conflict

- **Colonial Legacy**: The roots of ethnic tensions in Sri Lanka can be traced back to British colonial rule (1815-1948), which favored Tamils for government positions and education. After independence, policies favoring the Sinhalese majority, such as making Sinhala the sole official language in 1956, marginalized Tamils and fueled resentment.

- **Formation of the LTTE**: In 1976, Velupillai Prabhakaran founded the LTTE to fight for Tamil rights and autonomy. The group initially consisted of a small number of militants but grew rapidly in response to state repression and violence against Tamils.

## Key Events of the Civil War

### Escalation of Violence

- **Black July (1983)**: The civil war officially began on July 23, 1983, after an ambush by the LTTE that killed 13 soldiers. This incident triggered widespread anti-Tamil riots, resulting in hundreds of deaths and displacing thousands. The violence galvanized support for the LTTE among Tamils and marked a significant escalation in the conflict.

### Major Phases of War

- **Eelam War I (1983-1987)**: This phase saw intense guerrilla warfare as the LTTE launched attacks against government forces. The government responded with military operations aimed at suppressing the insurgency.

- **Indian Intervention (1987-1990)**: Following escalating violence, India intervened militarily under the Indo-Sri Lanka Accord to disarm the LTTE. However, this led to further conflict as the LTTE rejected disarmament.

- **Eelam War II (1990-1994)**: Renewed fighting occurred after Indian troops withdrew. The LTTE expelled thousands of Muslims from northern areas and continued its campaign against government forces.

- **Eelam War III (1995-2001)**: The Sri Lankan military launched major offensives against the LTTE. In December 1995, government forces captured Jaffna after a bitter battle. The LTTE retaliated with high-profile attacks, including bombings in Colombo.

### Ceasefires and Renewed Fighting

- **Ceasefire Agreement (2002)**: A ceasefire was established between the government and LTTE after Norwegian mediation. However, peace talks faltered in 2003 as both sides resumed hostilities.

- **Final Offensive (2008-2009)**: In early 2009, the Sri Lankan military launched a decisive offensive against the LTTE. By May 2009, government forces declared victory after capturing the last stronghold of the LTTE in Mullaitivu.

## Aftermath

The civil war resulted in an estimated 70,000 to 80,000 deaths and left deep scars on Sri Lankan society. The conflict displaced hundreds of thousands and caused significant humanitarian crises. Issues such as ethnic reconciliation, human rights violations during the war, and political instability remain contentious topics in post-war Sri Lanka.

### Legacy

The end of the civil war has not fully resolved ethnic tensions in Sri Lanka. Efforts toward reconciliation and rebuilding continue as the nation grapples with its complex legacy of conflict and division. The LTTE's legacy also influences contemporary discussions about governance, minority rights, and national identity within Sri Lanka.
