## History of Building Energy Modelling

The history of building energy modeling has evolved significantly over the decades, driven by technological advancements, regulatory changes, and an increasing emphasis on sustainability. Below is a structured overview of this evolution.

### Early Developments (1960s - 1980s)

- **Initial Models**: The origins of building energy modeling can be traced back to the 1960s when early computer-based models began to emerge. These models primarily focused on energy assessments and heating/cooling load calculations. Notable early simulation engines included BLAST and DOE-2, which laid the groundwork for future developments in energy modeling.

- **Energy Crisis Impact**: The energy crisis of the 1970s intensified efforts to reduce building energy consumption, leading to the establishment of building energy standards and regulations. This period marked a shift towards more systematic approaches to modeling energy use in buildings, with a focus on improving efficiency.

### Advancements in Simulation Software (1990s - 2000s)

- **Emergence of Building Energy Simulation Software**: The 1990s saw the development of sophisticated building energy simulation software such as EnergyPlus, eQUEST, and TRNSYS. These tools allowed for more accurate predictions of building energy performance by considering various factors like occupancy patterns, HVAC systems, and lighting.

- **Integration into Design Processes**: As sustainability gained prominence, energy modeling became an integral part of the architectural design process. An integrated design approach encouraged collaboration among architects, engineers, and energy modelers to optimize building performance from the outset.

### Regulatory Changes and Standardization (2000s - Present)

- **Standard Assessment Procedure (SAP)**: In the UK, the introduction of SAP in 1993 provided a standardized method for assessing the energy performance of domestic buildings. This procedure has undergone several revisions to improve accuracy and adapt to changing regulations related to energy efficiency.

- **Building Performance Evaluation**: The late 2000s saw an increased emphasis on performance-based building codes that required compliance with specific energy performance standards. Energy modeling became essential for demonstrating compliance with these codes and for certifications such as BREEAM (Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment Method) and LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design).

### Recent Trends and Future Directions (2010s - Present)

- **Whole-Building Life Cycle Assessment**: Recent advancements have expanded energy modeling beyond operational energy use to include embodied energy assessments throughout a building's life cycle. This holistic approach considers materials, construction processes, maintenance, and decommissioning impacts on overall sustainability.

- **Digital Twins and Smart Technologies**: The rise of digital twin technology allows for real-time monitoring and optimization of building performance through integration with IoT devices. These virtual replicas enable predictive maintenance and operational efficiency improvements.

- **Focus on Historic Buildings**: There is growing interest in applying energy modeling techniques to historic buildings. However, challenges remain regarding the accuracy of predictive models due to variations in traditional construction methods and materials. Research continues to refine these models to better assess the energy performance of older structures while preserving their historical integrity.

This overview highlights how building energy modeling has transformed from basic estimation tools into sophisticated systems that play a crucial role in sustainable architecture and urban planning. As technology continues to evolve, so too will the methodologies used to model and optimize building performance in response to environmental challenges.
