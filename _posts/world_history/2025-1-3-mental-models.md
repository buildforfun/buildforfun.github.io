Mental models



**Catalysts:** Substances that increase the rate of a chemical reaction without being consumed or altered themselves, by providing an alternative pathway with lower activation energy.
**Thermodynamics:**
1. **Closed system:** A system that does not exchange matter with its surroundings, but can exchange energy.
2. **Orderliness needs to be maintained:** In isolated systems, the natural tendency is for disorder (entropy) to increase over time, so orderliness must be actively maintained through the input of energy.
3. **Energy must be put in to maintain a clean place:** Keeping a system organized and free of disorder (low entropy) requires the continuous input of energy to counteract the natural tendency towards increasing entropy.
**Activation Energy:** The minimum amount of energy required to initiate a chemical reaction or cause a specific transformation or change in a system.
**Alloying:** The process of combining two or more metallic elements to create a new material (alloy) with enhanced or desirable properties compared to the individual components.
**Distributions:** The way data points or values are spread or distributed across a range, often described by probability distributions like normal, binomial, or Poisson distributions, which can help model and analyze real-world phenomena.
**Sampling:** The process of selecting a subset of individuals or observations from a larger population, with the goal of making inferences or estimations about the characteristics of the entire population based on the sample.

**Randomness:** The lack of pattern or predictability in a sequence of events or data, often associated with concepts like random variables, random processes, and stochastic models in probability theory.

**Equivalence:** The state of being equal or having the same value, often represented by the equal sign (=) in equations or expressions.

**Global and local maxima:** In calculus and optimization, a global maximum is the highest or largest value a function can attain over its entire domain, while a local maximum is the highest value within a specific interval or neighborhood of the domain.

**Margins of safety:** The margin of safety is an important component of some decisions and life. You can think of it as a reservoir to absorb errors or poor luck.

### Systems

**Churn:** Churn refers to the cyclical process of turnover, where components within a system are continuously replaced or renewed. In biology, it often describes the recycling of nutrients or materials through an ecosystem. In business, it can refer to the rate at which customers join and leave a service over time.

**Algorithms:** A set of well-defined rules or instructions for solving a problem or performing a computation, often used in computer systems and mathematical models to process data and make decisions.

**Critical mass:** The minimum amount or threshold of resources, participants, or conditions required for a system or process to become self-sustaining and continue operating or growing without external support.

**Irreducibility:** The property of a system where its behavior or functionality cannot be fully understood or replicated by studying or replicating its individual parts in isolation, as the system's behavior emerges from the interactions between its components.

**Law of diminishing returns:** As more resources or inputs are added to a system, the marginal gains or outputs eventually decrease, reaching a point where further additions produce negligible or even negative returns. This principle highlights the importance of finding an optimal balance in resource allocation within a system.

### Biology

**Natural selection and Extinction:** Natural selection is the process by which traits that enhance survival and reproduction become more common in a population over successive generations, while detrimental traits become rarer. Extinction occurs when a species dies out entirely due to inability to adapt to environmental changes or competition from other species.

**Adaptation and Red queen effect:** Adaptation refers to the evolutionary process where an organism becomes better suited to its environment over time. The Red Queen effect suggests that species must constantly adapt and evolve not just to gain reproductive advantage, but also to survive against ever-evolving competing species and environmental pressures.

**Ecosystems:** An ecosystem is a community of living organisms interacting with each other and their physical environment, exchanging energy and matter in a sustainable cycle.

**Niches:** A niche is the unique role and position a species occupies within an ecosystem, defined by how it meets its needs for food, shelter, reproduction, and other essentials.

**Self-Preservation:** The fundamental drive in living organisms to protect themselves from harm and maintain their existence, often through behaviors like fleeing danger, defending territory, or seeking resources.

**Replication:** The process by which living organisms make copies of themselves, passing on their genetic material to future generations through reproduction.

**Cooperation:** Mutually beneficial interactions between organisms, such as symbiotic relationships, group hunting, or raising offspring together, which increase the chances of survival.

**Hierarchical Organization:** The arrangement of biological systems into nested levels, from molecules to cells, tissues, organs, organisms, populations, and ecosystems, with each higher level emerging from the interactions of the lower levels.

**Tendency to minimize energy output:** Living organisms generally aim to conserve energy and minimize unnecessary expenditure, as energy is a limited resource required for essential functions like growth, reproduction, and survival.

**Activation energy:**  
1. Minimum energy needed to start a chemical or biological process.
2. The amount of effort or energy input required to initiate a reaction or change.
3. Catalysts can lower the activation energy barrier, speeding up reactions without being consumed themselves.

**Catalyst:** A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being consumed or altered itself, by providing an alternative pathway with lower activation energy.

**Chain reaction - Critical mass:** A self-sustaining sequence of events, where each event triggers the next, resulting in an exponential increase or propagation. Critical mass refers to the minimum amount of a substance or condition required to initiate and sustain the chain reaction.

**Homeostasis:** The tendency of biological systems to maintain a relatively stable internal environment, even when external conditions change, through coordinated physiological processes and feedback mechanisms.


**Opportunity costs:** The value of the next best alternative foregone when making a choice or decision. Considering opportunity costs helps us make rational economic decisions by weighing the benefits against what must be given up.

**Creative destruction:** The process by which new innovations and technologies disrupt and replace existing industries and firms. While disruptive in the short-term, it drives economic progress and growth in the long run.

**Comparative advantage:** The ability of an individual, firm, or country to produce a good or service at a lower opportunity cost than others. Specializing in areas of comparative advantage and trading allows for greater economic efficiency.

**Specialization:** Focusing production on a narrow range of goods or services, rather than trying to be self-sufficient. Specialization increases productivity and efficiency through economies of scale and the division of labor.

**Trademarks, patents, and copyrights:** Legal protections that grant exclusive rights to intellectual property, incentivizing innovation by allowing creators and inventors to profit from their ideas.

**Double-entry bookkeeping:** An accounting system that records each transaction as both a debit and a credit, providing a clear picture of a firm's financial position and performance.

**Utility:** The satisfaction or benefit derived from consuming a good or service. Marginal utility refers to the additional satisfaction from one more unit, which typically diminishes as consumption increases.

**Bribery:** The offering of money or favors to influence decisions, which distorts market incentives and undermines fair competition.

**Arbitrage:** The simultaneous buying and selling of an asset to take advantage of price differences across markets, helping to equalize prices and increase market efficiency.

**Supply and demand:** The relationship between the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing to sell (supply) and the quantity that consumers are willing to buy (demand) at various prices.

**Scarcity:** The fundamental economic problem of having unlimited wants but limited resources, necessitating choices about how to allocate those resources.

**Mr. Market:** A metaphor used by Benjamin Graham to personify the irrationality and mood swings of the stock market, reminding investors to be disciplined and not get caught up in market emotions.

**Seeing the front:** Having a clear understanding of the battlefield situation, including enemy positions, terrain, and potential avenues of attack or retreat. This situational awareness is crucial for effective military planning and decision-making.

**Asymmetric warfare:** A conflict where the opposing forces employ vastly different strategies, tactics, and resources, often with one side using unconventional or guerrilla tactics to offset the other's conventional military superiority.

**Two-front war:** A military conflict in which a nation or alliance must simultaneously defend against or engage enemies on two distinct fronts or geographic areas, dividing its resources and complicating strategic planning.

**Counter-insurgency:** Military and political efforts to defeat an insurgency, often involving a combination of combat operations, civil affairs, and addressing the root causes that fuel the insurgent movement.

**Mutually assured destruction (MAD):** A military doctrine during the Cold War based on the idea that a full-scale nuclear attack by one superpower would be deterred by the certainty of an equally devastating retaliatory strike, resulting in the complete destruction of both sides.

**Trust:** We tend to trust people who are similar to us or belong to our in-group, and distrust those who are different or belong to an out-group. This bias can lead to unfair judgments based on superficial characteristics rather than objective evidence.

**Bias from incentives:** Our judgments can be influenced by personal incentives or conflicts of interest, leading us to rationalize decisions that benefit us, even if they are unethical or harmful to others.

**Pavlovian association:** We may form associations between neutral stimuli and positive or negative experiences, leading to judgments based on conditioned responses rather than rational evaluation.

**Tendency to feel envy and jealousy:** Feelings of envy or jealousy towards others' success or possessions can lead to negative judgments and resentment, even when the person being judged has done nothing wrong.

**Tendency to distort due to liking/loving or disliking/hating:** Our judgments can be distorted by strong positive or negative emotions towards someone, leading us to overlook their flaws or exaggerate their shortcomings, respectively.

**Availability heuristic:** We tend to judge the likelihood of events based on how easily examples come to mind, rather than objective probabilities, leading to biased judgments.

**Representativeness heuristic:** We judge the likelihood of an event or situation based on how well it matches our preconceived stereotypes or mental models, rather than considering base rates or other relevant information.

**Failure to account for base rates:** We may neglect to consider the base rate or prior probability of an event when making judgments, leading to inaccurate conclusions.

**Stereotyping:** We often rely on oversimplified generalizations or stereotypes about groups of people, leading to biased and unfair judgments of individuals within those groups.

**Failure to see false conjunctions:** We may judge the likelihood of two events co-occurring as more probable than the likelihood of either event occurring alone, even when this violates the rules of probability.

**Social proof:** We tend to judge something as correct or appropriate based on whether others are doing it, rather than evaluating it objectively.

**Narrative instinct:** We have a tendency to construct coherent narratives to explain events, which can lead to biased judgments that fit our preconceived stories rather than the facts.

**Curiosity instinct:** Our innate curiosity about others can lead us to make judgments based on incomplete or unreliable information, in order to satisfy our desire to understand.

**Language instinct:** The way we use language and the words we choose can shape our judgments, as language is not a neutral medium but carries implicit biases and assumptions.

**First conclusion bias:** We often form initial judgments quickly based on limited information, and then fail to update or revise those judgments even when presented with new, contradictory evidence.
