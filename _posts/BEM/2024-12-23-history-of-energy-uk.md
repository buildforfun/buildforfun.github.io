# Comprehensive History of Energy in the UK

The history of energy in the United Kingdom is characterized by significant transformations in energy sources, technological advancements, and evolving policies aimed at addressing climate change. This overview will delve into the evolution of energy production, with a particular focus on the electricity grid and the changing renewable energy mix.

## Early Energy Sources (Pre-19th Century)

- **Wood and Peat**: For centuries, wood was the primary energy source for heating and cooking, while peat was utilized in areas with limited wood resources.
- **Coal**: The use of coal began to rise significantly in the 16th century, particularly with the advent of steam power during the Industrial Revolution.

## The Industrial Revolution (18th-19th Century)

- **Steam Engines**: The introduction of steam engines transformed industries and transportation, leading to a surge in coal demand.
- **Electricity Generation**: The first public electricity generator was installed in Godalming, Surrey, in 1881, marking the beginning of electricity as a significant energy source.

## Formation of the National Grid (20th Century)

### Initial Development

- **Electricity (Supply) Act 1926**: This act aimed to address the fragmented electricity supply industry. It led to the creation of the Central Electricity Board (CEB) and established a national grid system.
- **National Grid Launch**: The UK's first synchronized national grid began operating in 1933 at 132 kV, connecting 122 efficient power stations across Britain, allowing for more reliable and cheaper electricity supply.

### Expansion and Upgrades

- **Supergrid Development**: By the 1960s, advancements allowed for higher transmission voltages. The grid was upgraded to include 275 kV lines and later to 400 kV, creating what is known as the "Supergrid." This facilitated long-distance transmission from remote power stations to urban centers.
- **Integration and Efficiency**: The grid became fully integrated by 1938, allowing for better resource allocation during peak demands. It proved its resilience during World War II by redistributing power when local stations were damaged.

## Post-War Era and Nationalization

- **Electricity Act 1947**: This act nationalized the electricity supply industry, consolidating control under the British Electricity Authority. Significant investments in infrastructure and technology followed.
- **Central Electricity Generating Board (CEGB)**: Established to manage generation and transmission, CEGB oversaw major developments in nuclear power and coal-fired generation throughout the mid-20th century.

## Transition to Renewable Energy (Late 20th Century)

- **Environmental Awareness**: By the late 20th century, concerns over climate change prompted a shift towards renewable energy sources. The UK's first wind farm opened in Cornwall in 1991.
- **Renewables Obligation (2002)**: This policy required electricity suppliers to source a percentage of their energy from renewable sources, fostering growth in wind and solar energy sectors.

## Changes in Renewable Energy Mix (21st Century)

The contribution of renewable energy to the UK's energy mix has seen dramatic changes over recent years:

### Growth of Renewables

- **Percentage Increase**: The share of renewables in the UK’s energy mix increased from **10.7% in 2014** to **39.5% in 2023**, reaching **43.1% by September 2024**.

### Breakdown of Renewable Sources (September 2024)

As of September 2024, the renewable energy mix consisted of:

- **Wind Energy**: **30.4%**
- **Biomass**: **6.6%**
- **Solar Energy**: **4.7%**
- **Hydro Power**: **1.4%**

In contrast, fossil fuels accounted for only **25%**, primarily from gas (**24%**) and coal (**1%**) .

## Drivers Behind Renewable Growth

Several factors have contributed to this significant shift towards renewable energy:

1. **Government Policies**: Initiatives like the Renewables Obligation and subsequent support schemes have incentivized investments in renewable technologies.
2. **Technological Advancements**: Improvements in wind turbine efficiency and solar panel technology have reduced costs and increased capacity.
3. **Public Awareness**: Growing environmental concerns have led to increased public support for sustainable energy solutions.
4. **Climate Change Commitments**: The UK government has committed to achieving net-zero emissions by **2050**, with specific targets for decarbonizing electricity generation by **2035**.

## Future Goals

To meet its climate targets, the UK government has set ambitious goals for renewable energy capacity:

- Increase wind capacity from current levels (~11 GW) to **50 GW by 2030**.
- Expand solar capacity from approximately **14 GW** to **70 GW by 2035** .

## Conclusion

The history of energy in the UK illustrates a remarkable transformation from reliance on fossil fuels to a diversified mix that increasingly incorporates renewables. With ongoing investments and commitments toward sustainability, the UK is positioned to lead in renewable energy development while addressing climate change challenges effectively. Understanding these dynamics is crucial as the country strives for a sustainable energy future that balances economic growth with environmental stewardship.
