## History of Building Energy Modelling

### Early Developments (1960s - 1980s)

- **Initial Models**: 
  - The origins of building energy modeling date back to the 1960s with the emergence of early computer-based models.
  - These models primarily focused on energy assessments and heating/cooling load calculations.
  - Notable early simulation engines included **BLAST** and **DOE-2**, which established foundational principles for future developments.

- **Energy Crisis Impact**:
  - The energy crisis of the 1970s (petrol shortages) heightened the urgency to reduce building energy consumption.
  - This period led to the establishment of building energy standards and regulations.
  - A systematic approach to modeling energy use in buildings emerged, emphasizing efficiency improvements.

### Advancements in Simulation Software (1990s - 2000s)

- **Emergence of Building Energy Simulation Software**:
  - The 1990s witnessed the development of advanced simulation software such as:
    - **EnergyPlus**: A robust engine developed by the U.S. Department of Energy for detailed thermal and energy analysis.
    - **eQUEST**: A widely used free software for detailed building energy analysis, including lifecycle cost analysis.
    - **TRNSYS**: A versatile tool for transient thermal and energy performance analysis in buildings.
    - **DesignBuilder**: A user-friendly software that combines powerful simulation capabilities with intuitive design tools.
    - **OpenStudio**: An open-source platform developed by NREL that supports parametric modeling and integration with other tools.
    - **IESVE (Integrated Environmental Solutions Virtual Environment)**: A comprehensive suite designed for detailed building energy modeling and optimization.
    - **Autodesk Insight**: A cloud-based tool that integrates with Autodesk Revit for advanced energy analysis.
    - **EnergyPlus+IDA (Integrated Design Assistant)**: Combines EnergyPlus with optimization tools for comprehensive performance assessment.

- **Integration into Design Processes**:
  - As sustainability gained traction, energy modeling became integral to architectural design.
  - An integrated design approach fostered collaboration among architects, engineers, and energy modelers to optimize building performance from the outset.

### Regulatory Changes and Standardization (2000s - Present)

- **Standard Assessment Procedure (SAP)**:
  - Introduced in the UK in 1993, SAP provided a standardized method for assessing domestic building energy performance.
  - This procedure has undergone multiple revisions to enhance accuracy and adapt to evolving energy efficiency regulations.

- **Building Performance Evaluation**:
  - The late 2000s marked an increased emphasis on performance-based building codes requiring compliance with specific energy performance standards.
  - Energy modeling became essential for demonstrating compliance with these codes and obtaining certifications like **BREEAM** and **LEED**.

### Recent Trends and Future Directions (2010s - Present)

- **Whole-Building Life Cycle Assessment**:
  - Recent advancements have broadened energy modeling to encompass embodied energy assessments throughout a building's life cycle.
  - This holistic approach considers materials, construction processes, maintenance, and decommissioning impacts on overall sustainability.

- **Digital Twins and Smart Technologies**:
  - The rise of digital twin technology enables real-time monitoring and optimization of building performance through IoT integration.
  - These virtual replicas facilitate predictive maintenance and enhance operational efficiency.

- **Focus on Historic Buildings**:
  - There is increasing interest in applying energy modeling techniques to historic buildings.
  - Challenges persist regarding predictive model accuracy due to variations in traditional construction methods and materials.
  - Ongoing research aims to refine these models for better assessment of older structures while preserving their historical integrity.
