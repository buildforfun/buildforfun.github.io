## Guide to work life
Having worked professionally for nearly 5 years, I thought it might be useful to put together some advice for others looking to get started in the professional workplace. A disclaimer, any advice I give should not be taken as truth, form your own views based on your own research. Without further ado here we go!

Applying
---
Firstly, when applying for a job role, ask yourself am I right for the role? And, perhaps more importantly, is the role right for you? These are the two different questions that an interview seeks to answer. Make sure you prepare by researching your likely responsibilities and the company's values. Your curiosity for the role should drive you to do this anyway.

The Interview
---
Once you have found a role you like and the company likes you, they will invite you for an interview. Not all interviewers ask the same questions. Rather than preparing for all possible questions, go through all your completed projects. Think about how your experience/projects shows different skills. Practice using these examples in questions by going through some interview questions. A useful framework for answering interview questions is STAR which stands for Situation, Task, Action and Result. This formula makes sure you hit all the points that the interviewer is looking for.

Near the end of the interview, the interviewer will ask - do you have any questions for us. That's where you bring out a long list of questions. Either remember all the questions or print out a sheet of questions. At the end of my interview, the interviewer said he felt like I had interviewed him. Try and not to go overboard, but asking lots of good questions shows interest and sets you apart!

Starting to work
----
If they offer the role and you accept - hooray!

It's worth spending sometime upfront time getting set up properly. This will help you preform at your best. Get the equipment you want and set up your comuter configurations and have all the necessary softwares. Once you are ready to contribute, don't volunteer for too many projects at once. You want to show you are keen, but you also want to show you can deliver. Take one or two projects and make sure you deliver them.

It's helpful to put in some time with seniors to really understand what the company does, make sure you ask things you wouldn't find from a simple google search. This meeting is especially useful if you are working remotely.

As you learn more, you should slowly start to see how the work all fits in the overall structure. What does the company do? How does it generate money? What is the company organisation chart? How does the work provide value to society?

Start a repo of your notes. Writing notes will take you away from delivering things quickly but will pay many dividends many times over in the future. Your brain is good at having ideas and problem solving, but not so good at retaining vast amounts of information.

During projects
----
At the start you will suck. You lack experience and skill to be proficient. Ask if you are stuck, don't wait around. The important thing is to show you have given it a good go before you ask. When you do ask make sure you ask good questions, and remember to take notes, make sure you don't have to keep asking the same questions. As you complete projects, keep detailed notes on what you did and how you solved problems. Spend time learning, not just trying to get through work as soon as you can. Remember keep on learning!
