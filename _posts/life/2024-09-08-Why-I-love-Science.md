## Why I love science

Growing up, my parents told me that we were hindu. I like most kids do trusted the parents and this is what I believed in for many years. To be honest, we didn't know differently. We sort of assumed this was the default and never thought about it much. I did experience a feeling of being part of a community which I liked a lot at the time. The sculptures were pretty cool, but the stories my mum would tell me about the hindu gods were the most intriguing. However, one day, my mum told me this story about how this diety got an elephant head. I won't go into the whole story, but when she said the god's dad found an elephant, cut off the elepahnt head and stuck it on god's neck, I called bullshit. The story made no sense to me, and when I asked my mum any more questions she just said it's the truth and not to question it. Since then, I have not been religious. Many years later, I watched this clip of Rick Gervais where he said something along the lines of if you take all the science books and burnt them, in a thousand years they would come back the same, wheras if you did the same to relgious books, they would come back different. This really resonated with me.

Going to school and being taught science, I started slowly understanding that you can ask science the many why questions and it could answer most of them. Before I go on, let's define what Science is. Science is the understanding of the universe based on empirical evidence. The empirical evidence is the key that sets science apart from other belief systems. There are cases where science can't answer the why question, and this is because more research is needed, but it doesn't try to claim something without evidence for it. For example, we don't know what happened before the big bang, but science doesn't try to explain it using some supernatural miracle. Instead it's an area where more research and discovery is needed.

Science also gives us a framework to acquire new knowledge - the Scientific Method. The stages of the scientific method are loosely:
1. Observation: An observation is made 
2. Questioning of observation: The observation is questioned to see if there any problems
3. Further research: The area is researched to find out if there is already any existing solutions.
4. Hypothesis: A hypothesis is formed based on the observation and research.
5. Predictions: A testable prediction is made.
6. Experiment: Experiments are carried out and data is collated
7. Data analysis: The data is analysed to identify any patterns.
8. Conclusion: The data is used to determine if the hypothesis is proven.
9. Peer review: The findings are submitted for other scientists to critically evaluate the findings.

Experiments need to be designed in a way that they are reproducible (the results are consistent when the experiment is repeated) and accurate (the results are close to the true value). The standard for the experiments make sure that science that is incorrect is found out quite quickly. I love that science allows others to test claims that are made before it is accepted.
 
I love that science gives you the tools to think clearly and to apply them to challenges humanity faces. Science forces you to visualise and understand complicated systems and to make judgements. Other subjects often teach you to memorise vast contents but don't test your ability to apply as much as science. In the modern era, most the knowledge is easily searchable digitally, so the ability to memorise content isn't as important as it once was.

Finally and most importantly, my main reason for loving science is, it's fun! It's fun to understand how things work properly. Having a grasp of how things work from the ground up gives me such a thrill. When i am able to explain how something works and see visually how things operate is so fun and thrilling.
